Growing up with immigrant parents, I was raised to be frugal. I barely ate out. I never chased having the nicest clothes. My family has always been averse to the American instinct to just buy the solution to all our little problems. We try our best to make do with what we have. 

> There is one glaring exception to my frugality: I've always had a penchant for tech products. I'm willing to spend a premium on the latest and greatest, justifying my materialism with the decreased quality of life a broken phone or computer could cause. It's a personal bias that could be applied by someone to any other good or service they value.

Thankfully, excessive spending was never an urge I had to fight. Saving was as natural as eating. 

I happened upon a Reddit comment introducing a book called Die With Zero. The synopsis is we should strive to die with zero dollars in our bank accounts. Since then I've internalized the Die With Zero message into my own worldview. 

Here's a [summary](https://aliabdaal.com/book-notes/die-with-zero/).

## the three resources
The book generalizes life having three desirable resources that vary with age:
- Time
- Health
- Money

In your 20s you have the best health you'll ever have lots of time, but don't have much money. 

As you start increasing your salary and accumulating wealth. You lose time once you have family obligations (which is arguably a more valid way to spend your time). 

In retirement you suddenly have time again and ideally money saved but not the same health you once did.

> It's like when you saved all your gems in Clash of Clans for a future upgrade you never ended up starting.

It's made me question.

## family
It might seem incredulous, to be so selfish to not leave your kids any money in an inheritance. 

When you die, you're kids will probably be at a point in their lives when that money doesn't result in a significant quality of life increase. They probably already have families and a house. The money you leave behind is just a nice bonus instead of something they desperately need.

When you're dead you don't even get to see the benefit of that money.

It's way more effective to give them the cash when they most need it. Paying for college. Buying their first used car. Giving some money for a downpayment on a house.



What's the point of hoarding all the money to not use it?


## it's hard to balance
In retirement the greatest expense as you get older is undoubtedly healthcare. Timing your retirement perfectly such that you'll die with exactly with zero is hard. It's exceedingly unlikely if you want to prepare for emergencies.


## acknowledging the privilege
Now I understand having this mindset is privileged. My parents sacrificed a lot for me to have a stable upbringing. Most people raising familes do not have the ability to even entertain financial independence.

But having privilege doesn't mean you can't enjoy it. Use it, but have understanding and empathy for those who aren't as privileged.

## conclusion
Die With Zero isn't a plan. Circumstances may change. I don't expect my parents do bless me with money beyond what they've already done. But it's a philosophy that will guide my actions, especially in my relative youth.  

Ruthlessly identify what matters to you and what doesn't.
