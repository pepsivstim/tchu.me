> Entering the meadow from the West Gate, it feels like the sky is painted from floor to ceiling as if God swept his sky-blue brush in a heavenly arc. The clouds are right there — as if a quick leap could propel me into the puffy mist.

A few miles north of Copenhagen sits Jægersborg Dyrehave, otherwise known as *Dyrehaven* ("The Deer Park"). Founded in the 17th century as a royal hunting ground by Christian V of Denmark, it's a four square mile patch of managed nature preserve for the public and titular animals to enjoy.

I spent a lot of time in the deer park during study abroad. It served as an unrelenting training partner for the 2024 Copenhagen Marathon. It was a refuge for my mind, but tested my body with its rolling hills and often fierce winds.

## my daily bread
I discovered Dyrehaven on one of my first runs in Denmark. That run's route grew to be one of my go-tos for marathon training.

The park is defined by a set of forests that surround a central meadow often filled with herds of grazing deer. On a hill at the center of that meadow sits a small stylish palace called the [Hermitage](https://www.kongehuset.dk/en/palaces-and-the-royal-yacht/the-hermitage-palace/), meant to host royal banquets for the monarch.

![The "Hunting Lodge"](/images/hermitage.png#medium)

The palace rests on a small hill that overlooks the Øresund strait separating Denmark and Sweden. It's still one of my favorite views in all my travels.

![View of the Øresund and the Swedish coast](/images/dyrehaven-oresund.png#large)

Down the hill and on the water is the town of Taarbæk. I paused countless runs here to stretch and listen to music at the shore. It was *the* vibe spot to marvel at the vast sky and expanse of water.

![the vibe spot](/images/oresund-quay.jpg#medium)

## Danish summers are superior
Although dark, grey, and seemingly lifeless in the winter, Dyrehaven's bare oak forests are a refuge from freezing rain and violent gales for people and animals alike.

But those cold winter months were entirely worth the bright, sunny, brisk summer months that followed. The trees sprouted green leaves and the dead grasses began growing again. The herds of deer that moved slowly to conserve energy in the cold now moved with more life and vigor than before. The *hygge* we'd held onto dearly could finally be cast away to embrace the lovely Scandinavian summer.

## shelter in a storm of light
Denmark is usually too far south to see the northern lights. A solar storm on May 11th 2024 changed that.

Close to midnight, the group chat for my apartment started buzzing with students saying they could see light in the night sky. I looked outside my window to see nothing.  However, after taking a photo I could confirm there was indeed solar activity. With little hesitation I set out by bike to the darkest spot in town.

![View from 162E Lundtoftevej](/images/lundtoftevej-aurora.jpg#medium)

I wasn't expecting too much. I had already checked off my bucket list item of aurora spotting in Iceland. I didn't think anything could beat that.

This time was different.

As I cycled on the path to the Hermitage, a faint glow began to blanket the sky. By the time I reached the top of the hill overlooking the water, it had become a shimmering cloud of green and purple. 

![](/images/hermitage-aurora.jpg#medium)

I made my way down to my usual vibe spot off the coast. By then the solar storm was raining in a blazing fury. Those spindly rays of light shimmered in a slow, fleeting dance.

![](/images/oresund-aurora.jpg#medium)

 I spent the next hour wandering around Dyrehaven craning my neck towards the sky. I got on a call with my friend across the strait in Lund, and we marveled together at the dazzling high energy atmospheric collisions. It was completely unlike what we had seen in Iceland. Colors I didn't even know could occur in nature shined in the night.
 
![](/images/dyrehaven-aurora.jpg#medium)

Perhaps some of the deer who wandered by could only wonder what in the night sky could be so enchanting.

## azure skies


Frequented by other runners, walkers, cyclists, equestrians, even roller skiers, Dyrehaven is a gem of a place for anyone looking for a taste of history, nature, water, and of course — deer.

I don't know when I'll be back in Denmark. But when I do, I'll be sure to pay Dyrehaven a visit.
